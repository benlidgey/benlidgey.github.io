How do we get the next generation of software developers?

Fundamental changes

Way back in 2013 I was involved in a steering group fort the Welsh Government looking at how the curriculum could be changed to improve Digital and IT skills in children. We discussed a lot of topics and made recommendations that fed into the Donaldson report and ultimately into curriculum changes.

The basic premise of the recommendations we made was that Digital skills are not something to be treated separately in this age. We don’t teach children to use a pen, but teach them to write in the context of other subjects. We need to be doing the same for children with their Digital skills. Many jobs these days mean using some sort of digital device (computers, tablets, phones, tills that look like tablets).

What about software developers?

As someone recruiting for software development roles for a small tech company we need people who have the skills to get started.

I went to the National Software Academy to talk to the 2nd year students about what to think about when changing Agile and DevOps processes. The students were generally interested, knowledgeable about the principles involved and had some good thoughts when we had some interactive sessions (using the 1–2–4-All method from Liberating Structures, a good way to give people some time to stop and think).

I continue to be impressed with the NSA approach to teaching students the required skills for developing software in a commercial way.

The actual office space (as it is run in an office-like environment, not lecture theatres) is well equipped with plenty of linked TV screens to show content, radio mics and also a throwable microphone for the workshop sessions.

At work

The same approaches can be worth doing in the work environment: bring in some students for placements. This gives them work experience which is great for the students, and helps the company to do what is morally right to support the local community and economy.

There are many benefits for us too, including:

Bringing in a fresh mind — someone who will come in and ask and challenge what we are doing and why. I’ve seen this work well before as it makes people stop and reflect, and can highlight habits that people are in without any real benefits anymore.

In order for placement students to be able to challenge they need to feel able to do so. This means we all need to feel “psychologically safe” to say what we think — another benefit to the whole team.

The people in the team can also get experience in leading and mentoring someone else, and take ownership of the work the placement student will be working on.

It is something we are keen to do, and already have a post on the Cardiff University job site.

Come and join us!
