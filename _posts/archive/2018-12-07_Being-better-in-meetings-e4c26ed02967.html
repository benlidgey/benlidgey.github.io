Being better in meetings

Productivity != Lots of Meetings

Work life can often be full of lots of meetings. While meetings can be productive, they can also be time-consuming and not progress work. People seem to equate lots of meetings with being productive or important.

Productivity in manufacturing relates to measuring the outputs. If a manufacturing process has produced 10 more widgets this week than last week then it is more productive. Imagine swapping meetings for widgets in the previous sentence: “If you have had 10 more meetings this week than last week then you are more productive”. Output is based on quantity, and not necessarily quality.

The problem is that this assumes a meeting delivers an outcome. In Agile working being productive is about delivering outcomes (delivering value to the customer). Therefore work needs to focus on outcomes, not productivity. A successful outcome is one that is of sufficient quality.

Having a meeting is not always the best way to achieve an outcome. Meetings need to be planned, defined and prepared for in order to achieve an outcome.

As part of a review of my own way of working, and looking at the overall culture within the organisation on the common ways of working, I decided to become better at achieving outcomes from meetings that I arrange or attend.

There are a load of useful blogs and articles which I read and have references at the bottom. From all of those I have drawn up this list of principles which I will follow, and will take feedback and challenges from others if I do not stick to them.

Meetings

Decision-making meetings

The outcome from a decision making meeting should be one or more decisions, made by the person who has the authority to make the decision. The decision should be recorded and communicated so that it is clear to everyone (inside and outside of the meeting) what the decisions are.

The decision-maker needs to be present, and with enough information to make the decision. This may need the right people in the meeting to present the information and advice, or that information should be available before the meeting in sufficient time to be read before the meeting.

  • Challenge a meeting if you feel there are people missing who can contribute or make the decision.
  • Challenge if there is not a clear decision and communication plan from the meeting.
  • If you are the decision-maker and feel you have the right information to make the decision outside of the meeting, then make the decision and cancel the meeting. Just make sure the decision is communicated appropriately.

Retrospectives

Retrospectives are a way to review and improve areas including team health, processes, or a delivery outcome. The areas for discussion should be identified in advanced so people can prepare their thoughts.

In the meeting identify the issues and the actions or tasks that will address those issues. There are various techniques in Liberating Structures that can help these discussions. The action plan is the outcome that really makes these meetings valuable.

  • Challenge if there is not a clear area for review defined before the meeting
  • Challenge if there is not a clear action plan or set of actions from the meeting.

Problem-solving and brainstorming meetings

Collaborating with others in real time can spark lots of different ideas. These works well with a group of people with diverse knowledge, skills, backgrounds, and opinions. Be aware of different personality types so that everyone can feel comfortable making their ideas and thoughts known. Again Liberating Structures has various techniques to help. A code of conduct that is clearly stated at the start helps people understand what they can say and how to treat others in the meeting.

The outcome should be an agreed way forward, or a set of actions on how to progress.

  • Challenge if there is not a clear topic or problem to tackle defined before the meeting
  • Challenge if there is not a clear action plan or set of actions from the meeting.

Planning meetings

Planning often works well in meetings, again if there is a diverse group that can challenge each other, and identify different perspectives on what needs to be done.

The planning meeting will require a clear agenda, defined outcomes and background material the attendees need to read beforehand (allowing more reflective people to think in their own time). The outcome will be a produced plan.

  • Challenge if there is not a defined agenda or background material
  • Challenge if there is not a clear action plan or set of actions from the meeting.

Status updates

Status updates can often be done outside of meetings. Doing this will allow people to read and digest the status updates at their own pace. These meetings are worthwhile if there are complex things to be communicated, or the audience need the option to ask questions.

  • Challenge meetings that are status update or information cascade meetings. There are other ways for this information to be communicated that may be more useful.

Meeting principles

There are several general principles that I will try to adhere to.

  • Meetings should have an agenda and purpose defined in advance. This should include desired outcomes or decisions that need to be made.
  • Everyone should read the agenda and supporting materials before the meeting. If people have not then find out why not, and discuss if it can be provided in a different way that would allow the preparation. Another technique is to block time at the start of t he meeting to review the agenda and background material with everyone. (Personally, this shouldn’t replace sending out the information beforehand)
  • The invitees should be the minimal group to reach the desired outcomes or decisions. If you are on a meeting invite and do not think you can contribute to the outcome, or there are others in the meeting that can make the decision on your behalf, then decline the invite. Forward to someone who you think can contribute in your place.
  • Review the agenda and desired outcomes and decisions at the start of the meeting. Be prepared to stop the meeting if the required people are not there. Make it clear if the meeting is operating under “Chatham House rules”. Make sure everyone understands this.
  • Close the lid — Unless you’re acting as the designated scribe, laptops should be closed, and phones should be off the table. Challenge people if you feel they are distracted and not contributing to the meeting. In this digital age, people can say they are taking notes on their laptop, so make sure they are engaged in the meeting and are not taking a sneaky look at emails instead.
  • Vote with your feet — leave if you don’t think you can contribute. Be respectful to the others in the meeting and make it clear that you do not think you can contribute. I don’t see this happening enough; people are sat in meetings looking bored and disengaged.
  • Speak freely and respect others right to express their ideas and opinions.
  • Actions and minutes must be published as soon as possible after the meeting. Ideally use a document sharing tool, such as a wiki, or even post-it notes, but not just emailed to everyone. This is so they are available for all to see, and for new people to see, and if necessary, for access to be controlled. Challenge if the minutes and actions are not communicated soon after the meeting.

Code of conduct

While I would hope a code of conduct should not be necessary, it can be a good reminder for people on expected behaviours and therefore a way to engage with everyone. Code of conduct from groups like OneTeamGov can provide a good template.

The Chatham House Rule is often mentioned in meetings, but I have not seen it clearly stated at the start of the meeting. The definition is here.

So far…

It is early days. So far I have tried to define agendas for each meeting, and to summarise the actions and assigned tasks at the end of meetings. I have yet to vote with my feet.
