S03E03 Stop to go faster

This week continues to be an exciting, fast paced journey. Sometimes though we all need to stop to move forward.

Sometimes it is easy to keep sprinting hard to make progress but all you are doing is slowing down. It has always been my argument to spending time in investing in techniques like automated testing, CI and CD.

Back to basics

One of the challenges at work is to look to improve how we develop and deliver software. All of this needs to happen while we are working hard to deliver a lot for a very large global client. The temptation is to make little changes to improve things. Sometimes that can work, but there is also the risk that it adds little layers of complexity on top of one another, increasing the entropy.

An example this week is looking at the user story flow. As we are a distributed team we use Jira to manage the backlogs, sprints and tracking of work in progress. The main workflow in use has statuses that track work through dev, QA and builds. It has been updated to capture information needed by the different teams. However there are always cases where the workflow doesn’t work. Instead of adding another transition to deal with that case I suggested we need to stop and look at the user needs for the workflows and information needed.

Release planning

Release planning is another way to stop and think. Think about what work needs to happen next, the dependencies between different changes and how we can organise changes to deliver value to the client regularly.

I read this article about user story mapping, which I have found to be a useful way of planning smaller incremental releases.

“User Story Mapping is Big Design Upfront!”
“We do Scrum, and…” — Episode 8medium.com

Strategy

Finally I want to define the longer term strategy for the team, looking at where we want to get to and then looking at the steps we need to make to get there.

There have been loads of good ideas raised in the 2 weeks since I have been there, and so we need to look at the benefits and risks of the ideas and how much value is in implementing them. A recent one was to use Semantic Versioning (which we do, but not explicitly). This should be a quick change as we are mostly there already.

A bigger idea was to increase test coverage. We had good discussions around the benefits of unit tests, and when they can be a smokescreen — lots of unit tests can be written which looks good, but they may not be actually testing the right things. Unit test results need to be made visible, with things like information radiators, otherwise the temptation is to not write them or to not fix broken ones. Making the builds break if the unit tests don’t pass is another example of stopping to go forwards — the builds will break, there will be frantic fixing of tests and builds (hopefully not deleting broken tests), and then things will start working and rework rates should drop.

The A Team were great at making (literally) a plan to escape (let’s skip over how a crack military unit can be captured every week, and fire so many shots and not kill anyone). However, there are various quotes about “no plan survives first contact with the enemy” and tweets like

that show the importance of a plan but also the need for principles, autonomy and empowerment. This is something I will be working on next week.
