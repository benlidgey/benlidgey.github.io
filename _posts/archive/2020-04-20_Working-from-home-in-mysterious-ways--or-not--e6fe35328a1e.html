Working from home in mysterious ways (or not)

This is another “thinking out loud” post, trying to bring together various thoughts and ideas whizzing around my head.

The COVID-19 pandemic has changed the way a lot of people work, with many working from home. There are the obvious challenges that have been talked about

  • different ways of communicating are needed
  • less face to face contact
  • distractions
  • setting a “working life” schedule
  • accepting there are changes needed when working from home, such as acceptance of distractions

I would like to highlight there is often more to those challenges than managing the tech.

People work in different ways

People work in many different (and sometimes mysterious) ways. There are a whole load of personality tests you can do (Myers-Briggs, Insight Profiles are just some), or have a think about who you are and how you like to work and behave. Also think about how other people tend to work, and even have discussions with them on their preferred ways of working. Do they have brief conversations and that’s it, do they want things written down or drawn, do they make decisions based on their initial thoughts or gut feeling, or are they the sort of person who goes away and analyses all the evidence before coming to a conclusion?

Thinking about how people behave, interact and prefer to be interacted with will help to tune the ways to communicate better.

There is generally less face to face contact, meaning a greater effort in using other forms of communication. This doesn’t always mean switching from talking to people to messaging them, but more awareness of the different ways to communicate. For example, having a quick voice call with someone might be an easier way to resolve a problem, perhaps with screen sharing. Messaging someone and expecting them to reply straightaway is not the same as talking them, some people need time to understand the question and compose their reply.

Similarly, some people are more visual, and or require more examples to understand a problem or scenario. Remote white-boarding can be more difficult. There are online tools that will allow remote white-boarding specifically (I’ve not tried any so can’t recommend any), and other tools, like Trello, that allow sharing of lists or cards (I use Trello for remote retrospectives).

Video conferencing or voice calls tends to favour those people who are happy to speak up, and even interrupt or speak over people. Other more introverted people may not feel comfortable in contributing if there are no visual clues they want to say something. People facilitating (or anyone on the call) need to make sure everyone can contribute.

Everyone needs to be engaged on voice or video calls, and not reading emails, working on other things, reading Twitter. This is basic professional behaviour, as people shouldn’t be doing that in a physical meeting anyway.

Use documentation

There are some more subtle changes that can be made to not only support working from home, but to improve communication, quality, productivity and engagement.

Writing things down or drawing diagrams can help everyone to understand problems and scenarios, and to engage in meetings. Tools like wikis are good for quickly documenting discussions and decisions, or using collaboration tools like Office 365 or Google Docs can provide that joint view.

Jeff Bezos apparently banned presentations in favour of briefing documents. The documents present the information in a concise way that can be read, processed and understood by people in their preferred way. Presentations are useful in particular scenarios but are often not easily referred back to once the talk is over. It can also make you think clearly about what you are trying to communicate.

In remote working, documentation can be more important as it is harder to just ask someone, or get them to show you. Sharing and agreeing the content of a meeting in a document provides that knowledge sharing.

The Agile manifesto says “Working software over comprehensive documentation” but that does not mean no documentation.

Documentation can allow for interaction between a broader scope of personalities. People who favour visual thinking can use documents, especially with collaboration tools, to describe and agree the problem. More introverted people, or those who need to reflect on discussions have the documentation to review later.

Documentation is also a great way of knowledge sharing. In a functioning team people need to be open and transparent in their work. Conversely, people use the lack of documentation to keep the details of the ideas to themselves, leading to power-grabbing, empire building and a less transparent approach.

How do we go about it?

Think about who you are writing for. What acronyms can you expect them to know, and what needs to be defined. GDS have some good tips on simple writing. One thing I have got in the habit of doing is using “such that” or “for example” instead of “e.g.” as it makes the text flow better.

Think about the people you are working with and how they would want to interact. Are there the rights tools, or would that over-complicate things? Keeping it simple is probably best.

Keep notes and make them open and shared.

Is there a mixed approach for remote working and collaboration? I did wonder about how some Liberating structures, such as “ 1–2–4-all” can be worked in a remote environment. Any ideas are welcome!

Keep documentation fresh and up to date. Old documentation can be more damaging than useful. People can take time to read it, and take actions that are wrong or not useful.
